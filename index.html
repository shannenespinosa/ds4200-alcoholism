Background

Alcohol consumption is often editorialized in college life. Sometimes, in the glitz and glamor of partying, it's hard to remember the severity of alcohol related diseases. The NIH estimates that approximately 30 million or 10% of Americans have alcohol addictive disorders. Alcohol can impact longevity and the quality of life of those intoxicated and those around them. So how does college play into a potentially life long comorbidity?

In our initial exploratory research into the surveillance of excessive alcohol use, we found reports indicating drastic decreases in life expectancies when comparing drinkers to non-drinkers (Fig 1) as well as insight into the non-linear relationship of alcohol consumption with all-cause mortality rates (Fig 2).

Figure 1.   The variance in life expectancy (measured in years) across ranges of excessive and limited levels of alcohol use. The controls for comparison are individuals who neither smoke nor drink1.

Figure 2.   The nonlinear association between alcohol intake and the risk of mortality from all causes of death follow a dose-response pattern with a J-shaped association, indicating initial decrease in risk, followed by steep increases as consumptions levels rise2.

With this in mind, our project seeks to analyze Alcohol-Related Disease Impacts (ARDIs) across the United States by looking into patterns of alcohol based deaths based on numerous factors that may suggest a higher prevalence of normalization of abusive or binge-drinking culture. We used Pandas, GeoPandas, D3, and Altair for our final analyses.

Dataset

We analyzed the dataset from Health.gov on Alcohol-Related Disease Impact. It contains 30 columns and 62,505 rows of data. The rows are data reports between 2015 and 2019 across ages and sexes for deaths attributable to alcohol. It contains estimates of alcohol-related disease impacts across the United States, noting causes of death and years of potential life lost.

The dataset, filtered_alc_mort.csv, documents total fatalities in a given combination of state, extent, cause of death, age group, gender, etc. For instance, between 2015 and 2019, there were 13 deaths documented concerning excessive alcohol use among females under 21 leading to declarations of alcohol poisonings as causes of death in West Virginia.

In summary, the most relevant features of the dataset we have analyzed are the following:

  • LocationDesc: String entries of the state wherein the ARDI was documented
  • AgeGroup: String entries of whether the individuals documented were of age ranges: 0-19, 20-34, 35-49, 50-64, and 65+
  • Fatalities: Integer entries of total fatality counts on a given documented incident combination
  • Cause_of_Death: String entries of declared cause of death
  • Sex: Sex as listed on Death Certificate

Lastly, our geographic analyses were mapped alongside state population data from Natural Earth Vector, a public domain map, allowing for more accurate state-by-state per capita comparisons across GeoPandas plot analyses.

Read more about the data collection methods for ARDIs in Alcohol and Public Health Methods at the CDC. Our analyses were inspired by the CDC's Application on Alcohol-Related Disease Impact and the CDC's Data on Excessive Drinking.

National Overview of ARDIs

The Alcohol-Related Disease Impact application, or ARDI, estimates the number of deaths and years of potential life lost due to alcohol consumption. They help inform policy and response for public health agencies and researchers regarding the impact of alcohol on population health. To do this, the ARDI method analyzes mortality data and alcohol consumption patterns to calculate the proportion of deaths attributable to alcohol-related causes, with factors such as age, sex, alcohol use, and more3.

Across all fifty states, the alcohol-related causes of death leading to the highest rates of fatality are: (1) alcoholic liver disease, (2) poisoning, not alcohol, (3) motor vehicle traffic crashes, (4) hypertension, (5) suicide, (6) liver cirrhosis, and (7) homicide (Fig 3). While rates of fatality nationwide drop sharply following the highest seven, the diverse range of health issues leading to death displayed here give insight into the multifaceted nature of alcohol-related harm. Particularly, not only may excessive alcohol most likely lead to liver damage, it also relates to impaired judgments in ingestion, causing poisoning, as well as impaired cognitive and motor functions, causing accidents.

With this foundational understanding of nationwide alcohol health issues, we find increased nuances surrounding the complex landscape of public health response when we break down the causes of death per state. We find geographic variations in alcohol-related mortality patterns (Fig 4). For instance, states like Massachusetts with better healthcare infrastructure and access to preventative services would have overall lower rates of alcohol-related mortality due to early intervention and treatment. In addition, the availability of transportation and walking infrastructure minimize the risk of alcohol-based motor vehicle crashes. This comparison is very evident when compared to states like Florida and Texas.

Case Study: Age

As an area of further analysis, we delved into age-specific patterns of alcohol-related fatalities across different states. In doing so, we can uncover nuanced dynamics of alcohol-related harm and better inform targeted interventions at a local level to address age-specific vulnerabilities in mitigating alcohol-related mortality risks.

In the geospatial choropleth map, we can compare alcohol-related deaths of various age groups across the different states (Fig 5). For instance, we see that Louisiana particularly exhibits the highest alcohol-related deaths among individuals aged 0-19. This provides insight into how Louisiana may need to explore and address concerning trends in socioeconomic disparities, accessibility to alcohol, and cultural norms surrounding alcohol consumption (such as Mardi Gras) and how they lead to prevailing risk-taking behaviors among adolescents. On the other hand, states like New Hampshire demonstrate among the lowest rates of alcohol-related deaths, especially for adolescents, which may reflect more supportive communities for youth and overall stronger prevention efforts against alcoholism.

On the other spectrum, for ages 65 and above, states like Oregon, Vermont, Maine, and Florida depict particularly elevated rates of alcohol-related deaths for that age group, noting limited supportive services and concerning cultural attitudes among aging populations. Notably, Utah is a clear outlier in this range, with especially low rates of alcohol-related deaths among the elderly. Given the state's stringent alcohol policies, they inevitably form community support systems that promote a healthy aging population.

For a more comprehensive overview of net fatality rates across all age groups, the heatmap relays insights into distinct regional patterns we could not observe in the geospatial choropleth map (Fig 6). When fully compared, we see how states like California, Florida, and Texas consistently exhibit highest net fatalities, reflective of the states' larger population sizes and urbanization likely leading to greater accessibility to alcohol, more complex socioeconomic disparities, and more limited healthcare access and enforcement of alcohol-related policies simply due to the population.

Thus, in focusing on an age-specific case study, we are able to compare the fatalities and their regional variances, emphasizing once more the multifaceted nature of alcohol-related harm and the importance of locally directed public health strategies and community-based interventions in mitigating alcohol-related morbidity.

It is evident that there is a gender gap between the sexes in Alcohol Mortality Rates. Despite Women being the Majority of the US population, men have an almost 2:1 mortality rate across almost every state. While the reason behind this phenomena cannot be claimed based on this data alone, there are some potential hypothesis. Primarily, men are promoted hard liquor such as wiskey and bourbon. Whereas cocktails are often seen as more feminine. Direct alcohol content may contribute to some increased acute moralities. However, this rationale cannot be justified on this dataset alone are require further investigation.

Conclusion

In having uncovered in-depth the geographic nuances and disparities nationwide on alcohol-attributable deaths, we can fully understand the comprehensive view into how variable the causes of death state-by-state simply due to the aforementioned spectra of specialized regional vulnerabilities.

Going around different causes of death on the geospatial chloropeth map reveal further the extent of regional disparities (Fig 7). The western half of the United States show higher prevalences of alcoholic liver disease and suicide, whereas the eastern half conversely highlight coronary heart disease and laryngeal cancer. Florida becomes an outlier for ischemic stroke fatalities, given the combination of large urban centers and challenges in healthcare access. Southern states like Louisiana, Mississipi, and Alabama have a high concentration of homicide fatalities, attributable to cultural influences in crime rates and access to firearms.

Finally, the comprehensive heatmap concludes our understanding of alcohol-related mortality patterns nationwide (Fig 8). We see alcoholic liver disease as the most significant burden across multiple states, emphasizing the pervasive impact of chronic alcohol abuse on liver health. To a lesser, yet still notable, extent, we see again suicide, homicide, hypertension, liver cirrhosis, motor vehicle traffic crashes, and poisoning across all fifty states, emphasizing once more the multifaceted nature of alcohol-related mortality.

Addressing such a myriad of underlying factors requires locally comprehensive public health interventions targeting substance abuse, mental health support, violence prevention, and even road safety measures. As a nation and in our own communities, we can prioritize evidence-based interventions, fostering cross-sector collaboration, and promoting public awareness and education to reduce the national burden of alcohol-related harm. In increasing health equity and preventing substance abuse, we can altogether create environments that support health behaviors and well-being for all.
